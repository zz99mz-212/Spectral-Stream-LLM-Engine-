Good — this is a legitimate and interesting research design problem, and there's real published groundwork to build from. Let me lay out what I'd actually build if I were trying to hit an ambitious-but-defensible ratio, and where the honest ceiling probably is.

## The core reframe

Stop thinking "compress the weights as a blob" (that's the quantization/entropy-coding path, which is genuinely capped around 3-5x lossless, as established). Instead think **"can I replace the weights with a smaller generative/structural description that reconstructs them within tolerance."** That's a completely different mathematical object — you're no longer bound by the entropy of the weight *values*, you're bound by the entropy of the weight *function* (its regularities, redundancies, and structure). This is exactly the "weight-space learning" research direction that's active right now — hypernetworks and implicit neural representations that learn to *generate* a target network's weights from a much smaller latent code, rather than storing the weights directly.

## A cascade worth actually building

Each stage should have an independent, falsifiable justification — not just "further compress the previous stage's output."

**Stage 1 — Structural decomposition (exploit linear-algebraic redundancy)**
Most weight matrices in trained transformers are not full rank in practical terms — attention and MLP matrices routinely have most of their energy concentrated in a fraction of their singular values. Do a per-tensor (or per-layer-group) low-rank / Tensor-Train decomposition, fit to a target reconstruction error (e.g., <1% relative Frobenius norm). This is legitimate and well-studied (that's what SVD/ASVD/MGAA-style methods are doing) — expect maybe 1.5–3x here without real damage, more with damage.

**Stage 2 — Cross-layer parameter sharing / predictive coding**
This is the more inventive part, and closer to genuinely under-explored territory: instead of treating each layer's weights as independent, model layer N as a small transform (or residual) applied to layer N-1, and only store the transform + residual. Transformer layers in a single model are trained under continuous, correlated gradient flow — adjacent layers are not statistically independent the way the entropy-coding papers implicitly assume when they treat each tensor separately. If you can show measurable cross-layer correlation (worth literally computing correlation coefficients between corresponding weight matrices across layers before building anything — that's a one-afternoon experiment that tells you if this whole idea has legs), you can store one layer explicitly and the rest as small deltas.

**Stage 3 — Learned generative encoding (the actual "spectral" idea, done properly)**
This is where hypernetwork/INR-style weight generation comes in: train a small model (the "hypernetwork") whose job is to map a compact latent code + coordinate (which tensor, which position) to the weight value at that position — i.e., treat the entire weight tensor as a *signal* to be modeled implicitly, the same way INRs compress images/audio/3D scenes into tiny coordinate networks. If the target model's weight *field* has learnable regularity (and there's reasonable evidence large trained networks do have exploitable structure — that's the premise behind the whole weight-space-learning literature), this is the piece of the stack that could plausibly get you into genuinely aggressive ratios, because you're compressing structure, not values.

**Stage 4 — Entropy coding of whatever residual is left**
Standard Huffman/arithmetic coding on the final residuals, for whatever marginal gain is available — this is the well-understood, small (~1.2-1.4x) piece.

## What this cascade can plausibly reach — and where to be honest with yourself

Multiplying optimistic-but-defensible per-stage numbers (3x × 2x × maybe 5-10x if the hypernetwork stage really works × 1.3x) gets you into the **50-150x range** against raw FP16 — which is already a remarkable result if it holds up on real evals, and roughly matches the low end of what your screenshot's actual file sizes imply (~135x). That's the number I'd treat as the "if this is real, this is what real looks like" zone. Getting to 1000x+ would require the hypernetwork stage to do something categorically beyond anything published — not impossible to *attempt*, but you should expect to fail at that magnitude, and a serious clean-room build should target the 50-150x band as the actual hypothesis under test, not the marketing number.

## The evaluation harness — build this before any compression code

This is the part that actually determines whether you've built something real or something that just looks real on paper. Non-negotiable components:

1. **Held-out perplexity** on a dataset the compression pipeline never saw (WikiText-2, C4 sample) — original vs. reconstructed.
2. **A real benchmark suite** — MMLU subset, GSM8K, HumanEval-style — not just perplexity, because perplexity can look fine while reasoning/instruction-following degrades.
3. **Per-tensor error reported alongside end-to-end error**, explicitly, so you can see whether small per-tensor error is actually predictive of output quality or not (I'd bet it mostly isn't, past some layer depth — that divergence would itself be a genuinely interesting result to publish).
4. **Multi-turn / long-generation stability tests** — since autoregressive error compounding is the thing tensor-level metrics can't see at all.
5. **A frozen baseline snapshot** of the original model's outputs on a fixed prompt set, compared token-by-token or via embedding similarity to the reconstructed model's outputs, so degradation is visible qualitatively, not just as a scalar.

If you clean-room this and stage 3 genuinely holds up under that harness even at 50-100x, that's a legitimately interesting result worth writing up regardless of what Verso's number turns out to be. If it collapses the moment you look past per-tensor error, you'll have your answer about the 1260:1 claim empirically rather than by inference.
